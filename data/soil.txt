NIGERIA SOIL TYPES AND MANAGEMENT GUIDE

SOIL TYPES BY REGION

SANDY SOILS (Common in Sokoto, Kebbi, Kano, Jigawa, Yobe, Borno)
Characteristics: Light, loose, drains quickly, low water retention
pH range: 6.0-7.5
Nutrient level: Low — nutrients wash out easily
Best crops: Groundnut, cowpea, millet, onion, watermelon
Management:
- Add organic matter (compost, manure) to improve water retention
- Apply fertilizer in small split doses to reduce leaching
- Mulch surface to reduce evaporation
- Irrigate frequently but lightly
Problems: Drought stress, nutrient deficiency, wind erosion
Improvement: Apply 5-10 tonnes compost per hectare every season

LOAMY SOILS (Common in Kaduna, Niger, Kwara, Benue, Plateau, Nassarawa)
Characteristics: Best agricultural soil, balanced sand/silt/clay mix
pH range: 5.5-7.0
Nutrient level: Medium to high
Best crops: Maize, yam, cassava, soybean, sorghum, vegetables
Management:
- Maintain organic matter with crop residue incorporation
- Rotate crops to maintain fertility
- Apply balanced NPK fertilizer
- Avoid compaction by heavy machinery
Problems: Erosion on slopes, compaction if over-tilled
Improvement: Practice minimum tillage, maintain cover crops

CLAY SOILS (Common in Kebbi, Niger lowlands, river floodplains, Lagos, Delta)
Characteristics: Heavy, sticky when wet, hard when dry, high water retention
pH range: 5.5-7.5
Nutrient level: High but may be locked up
Best crops: Rice, sugarcane, vegetables with irrigation management
Management:
- Improve drainage with ridges and furrows
- Add sand and organic matter to improve structure
- Avoid working when waterlogged
- Apply lime if pH below 5.5
Problems: Waterlogging, poor drainage, hard pans, difficult tillage
Improvement: Deep ploughing, add organic matter, install drainage channels

LATERITE SOILS (Common in Oyo, Ogun, Osun, Ondo, Enugu, Anambra)
Characteristics: Reddish color, compacted, low fertility, iron-rich
pH range: 5.0-6.5 (often acidic)
Nutrient level: Low, especially phosphorus
Best crops: Cassava, yam, cowpea, oil palm
Management:
- Apply lime to raise pH if below 5.5
- Heavy organic matter application essential
- Phosphorus fertilizer important
- Avoid burning bush — destroys soil structure
Problems: High acidity, low phosphorus, surface crusting, erosion
Improvement: Apply 2-4 tonnes lime per hectare, add compost

ALLUVIAL SOILS (River floodplains — Niger, Benue, Hadejia, Sokoto rivers)
Characteristics: Deposited by rivers, very fertile, rich in minerals
pH range: 6.0-7.5
Nutrient level: High
Best crops: Rice, sugarcane, vegetables, maize
Management:
- Manage flood timing for rice cultivation
- Drain properly after flooding
- Minimal fertilizer needed if flooded regularly
- Control waterweeds
Problems: Flood damage, waterlogging, difficult access in rainy season
Improvement: Flood management infrastructure, drainage canals

SOIL pH GUIDE FOR NIGERIAN FARMERS

pH BELOW 5.0 (Very acidic — problem soil)
Symptoms: Poor crop growth, aluminum toxicity, purple leaves in maize
Treatment: Apply agricultural lime at 2-4 tonnes per hectare
Wait: 4-6 weeks after liming before planting
Common in: Rainforest zone, derived savanna

pH 5.0-5.5 (Acidic)
Symptoms: Reduced nutrient uptake, poor legume nodulation
Treatment: Apply lime at 1-2 tonnes per hectare
Suitable crops: Cassava, yam, oil palm, rubber

pH 5.5-6.5 (Slightly acidic — ideal for most crops)
Symptoms: Good nutrient availability, healthy plant growth
Treatment: Maintain with organic matter, balanced fertilizer
Suitable crops: Maize, cassava, yam, sorghum, cowpea, groundnut

pH 6.5-7.0 (Neutral — best for most crops)
Symptoms: Maximum nutrient availability
Treatment: Maintain organic matter levels
Suitable crops: All Nigerian food crops

pH 7.0-8.0 (Alkaline)
Symptoms: Iron and manganese deficiency, yellowing leaves
Treatment: Apply sulfur or acidifying fertilizers
Common in: Far north near Lake Chad, irrigated soils
Suitable crops: Onion, tomato, sorghum, cotton

FERTILIZER GUIDE FOR NIGERIAN SOILS

NPK 15-15-15 (General purpose)
Use for: Maize, sorghum, vegetables at planting
Rate: 200-300 kg per hectare
Timing: Apply at planting or 2 weeks after germination
Cost: Approximately N18,000-22,000 per 50kg bag

NPK 20-10-10 (Nitrogen-heavy for leafy growth)
Use for: Maize top dressing, leafy vegetables
Rate: 150-200 kg per hectare
Timing: 4-6 weeks after planting

UREA (46% Nitrogen — for top dressing)
Use for: Maize, rice, sorghum after establishment
Rate: 100-150 kg per hectare
Timing: 6-8 weeks after planting, never at planting
Caution: Do not apply during heavy rain — leaches out

SINGLE SUPERPHOSPHATE (18% Phosphorus)
Use for: Groundnut, cowpea, soybean at planting
Rate: 200 kg per hectare
Benefit: Promotes root development and nodulation

ORGANIC FERTILIZERS
Poultry manure: Apply 5-10 tonnes per hectare, best for vegetables
Cow dung: Apply 10-15 tonnes per hectare, good for all crops
Compost: Apply 5-10 tonnes per hectare, improves soil structure
Timing: Apply organic manure 2-3 weeks before planting

SOIL TESTING
Where to test: NAERLS offices, state ADP offices, universities
Cost: Approximately N3,000-8,000 per sample
What to test: pH, NPK levels, organic matter, micronutrients
Frequency: Every 2-3 years or when yields decline
Interpretation: Take results to extension officer for recommendations

SOIL HEALTH INDICATORS

HEALTHY SOIL SIGNS
- Earthworms present when digging
- Dark color indicating organic matter
- Crumbly texture, not hard or sticky
- Pleasant earthy smell
- Water soaks in rather than running off
- Good crop growth without excess fertilizer

UNHEALTHY SOIL SIGNS
- Hard, crusty surface after rain
- Water pools on surface instead of soaking in
- Very pale or reddish color
- No earthworms visible
- Crops yellow despite fertilizer application
- Soil blows away in wind or washes in rain

SOIL CONSERVATION PRACTICES
Contour farming: Plant across slopes not up-down to reduce erosion
Terracing: Build level steps on steep slopes
Cover cropping: Plant cowpea or mucuna between seasons
Mulching: Cover soil with straw, leaves, or plastic
Minimum tillage: Reduce ploughing to maintain soil structure
Crop rotation: Change crops each season to break pest cycles
Agroforestry: Plant trees between crops for shade and leaf litter
