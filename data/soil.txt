NIGERIA SOIL TYPES OVERVIEW

Nigeria has 6 main soil types distributed across its agro-ecological zones. Understanding your soil type is the first step to profitable farming.

---

SANDY SOIL (HAUSA: YASHI)
Characteristics: Light, loose, gritty texture. Does not hold water. Warms up quickly. Low nutrients.
Found in: Kano, Katsina, Sokoto, Zamfara, Kebbi, Borno, Yobe, Jigawa, Niger
Best crops: Groundnut, cowpea, sorghum, millet, melon, sesame, watermelon, cassava, sweet potato
Problems: Leaches nutrients quickly, dries out fast, low organic matter
How to improve sandy soil:
- Add compost or cow dung at 5-10 tonnes/ha every season
- Use mulch to retain moisture
- Apply fertilizer in small doses frequently (split 3-4 times)
- Plant legumes like cowpea as cover crop to add nitrogen
- Avoid over-irrigation which washes nutrients below root zone
Fertilizer recommendation: Apply NPK in 3 splits. Extra phosphorus (SSP) helps root development.
Irrigation need: High - water every 2-3 days
pH range: Usually 6.0-7.5
Investor note: Low land cost but high irrigation investment needed. Best for groundnut and sesame export.

---

CLAY SOIL (HAUSA: LAKA)
Characteristics: Heavy, sticky when wet, hard when dry. Holds water and nutrients well. Slow to warm.
Found in: Benue, Anambra, Cross River, Delta, Rivers, Bayelsa, Kogi, Kwara, Plateau lowlands
Best crops: Rice, yam, cassava, maize, soybean, sugarcane, vegetable crops
Problems: Waterlogging, poor aeration, hard to cultivate when dry, cracks in dry season
How to improve clay soil:
- Add sand and compost to improve drainage and aeration
- Build raised beds or ridges to improve drainage
- Never work clay soil when wet - damages structure
- Apply gypsum (calcium sulfate) at 1-2 tonnes/ha to improve structure
- Use deep-rooted cover crops like pigeon pea to break up hardpan
Fertilizer recommendation: Standard NPK works well. Avoid excess nitrogen which causes lodging in rice.
Irrigation need: Low - retains moisture well. Risk of waterlogging.
pH range: Usually 5.0-6.5
Investor note: Ideal for rice production. Benue, Niger, Kebbi river basin clays are highly productive.

---

LOAMY SOIL (BEST SOIL TYPE)
Characteristics: Mix of sand, silt, and clay. Good structure. Holds water and nutrients. Easy to cultivate.
Found in: Plateau, Kaduna, Nasarawa, Benue, Niger, Kwara, Oyo, Ogun, Ondo, FCT
Best crops: All crops perform well. Especially good for maize, tomato, pepper, yam, cassava, vegetables
Problems: Few problems. Can become compacted if heavy machinery used repeatedly.
How to maintain loamy soil:
- Apply compost annually to maintain organic matter
- Rotate crops to prevent nutrient depletion
- Minimize tillage to preserve soil structure
- Mulch between seasons
Fertilizer recommendation: Standard NPK recommendations for each crop
Irrigation need: Moderate - 2-3 times per week
pH range: Usually 6.0-7.0
Investor note: Premium land. Highest crop yields. Kaduna, Plateau, Oyo loamy soils are highly sought.

---

LATERITE/FERRALITIC SOIL (IRON-RICH RED SOIL)
Characteristics: Red or orange color from iron and aluminum oxides. Highly weathered. Low nutrients. Hardens when exposed.
Found in: Ondo, Ekiti, Ogun, Osun, Edo, Delta, Cross River, parts of Plateau
Best crops: Cassava, yam, oil palm, rubber, cocoa, cashew, citrus
Problems: Very low nutrients, highly acidic (pH 4.5-5.5), forms hard crust (hardpan), loses topsoil easily
How to improve laterite soil:
- Apply large amounts of organic matter (10-20 tonnes compost/ha)
- Lime application essential - 2-4 tonnes/ha to raise pH
- Minimum tillage to prevent erosion and hardpan formation
- Agroforestry - plant trees alongside crops
- Terracing on slopes to prevent erosion
Fertilizer recommendation: Phosphorus most limiting. Apply SSP 300 kg/ha. Lime before any fertilizer.
Irrigation need: Moderate. Drains quickly.
pH range: 4.5-5.5 (acidic - most crops need liming)
Investor note: Good for cocoa, cashew, rubber, oil palm which tolerate acidity. Low cost but high amendment input needed.

---

ALLUVIAL SOIL (RIVER VALLEY SOIL)
Characteristics: Deposited by rivers. Deep, fertile, rich in minerals. Found near river banks.
Found in: Niger River valley, Benue River valley, Sokoto River valley, Kebbi River areas, Cross River basin
Best crops: Rice, sugarcane, vegetables, maize, yam - very high yields
Problems: Flood risk during heavy rains. Some areas have high water table.
How to manage alluvial soil:
- Build flood control bunds during flood season
- Avoid planting in lowest-lying areas
- Take advantage of natural fertility - can reduce fertilizer by 25%
- Plant flood-tolerant varieties in flood-prone areas
Fertilizer recommendation: Soil test first. Often naturally high in nutrients. Supplement with potassium.
Irrigation need: Low to none - high natural moisture
pH range: 6.0-7.5
Investor note: Most productive soil in Nigeria. Kebbi, Niger, Benue river valleys are premium investment zones for rice and vegetables.

---

HYDROMORPHIC SOIL (FADAMA/WETLAND SOIL)
Characteristics: Waterlogged for part or all of year. Dark color from organic matter. Found in depressions and valleys.
Found in: Fadama lands across all states. Major fadama in Niger, Kebbi, Sokoto, Borno, Kano
Best crops: Dry season: tomato, pepper, onion, garlic, wheat. Wet season: rice, sugarcane
Problems: Poor drainage, anaerobic conditions, high iron and manganese toxicity in some areas
How to manage fadama soil:
- Use during dry season when water table drops
- Install drainage channels
- Raised bed cultivation during wet season
- Test for iron toxicity before planting rice
Fertilizer recommendation: Phosphorus often locked up. Apply SSP. Avoid urea - ammonia toxicity in waterlogged conditions.
Irrigation need: None in wet season. Pump irrigation in dry season from shallow water table.
pH range: 5.0-6.5
Investor note: Fadama land is most valuable dry season farmland in northern Nigeria. N200,000-N500,000/ha/year lease.

SOIL pH GUIDE FOR NIGERIAN FARMERS AND INVESTORS

pH scale: 0-14. Below 7 is acidic, 7 is neutral, above 7 is alkaline.
Most Nigerian crops grow best at pH 6.0-7.0.

pH below 5.0 (Very acidic):
- Toxic aluminum and manganese damage plant roots
- Most nutrients unavailable
- Only acid-tolerant crops: cassava, oil palm, rubber, pineapple
- Fix: Apply 4-6 tonnes/ha agricultural lime. Wait 4-6 weeks before planting.

pH 5.0-5.5 (Acidic):
- Phosphorus becomes unavailable
- Common in Ondo, Ekiti, Ogun, Cross River red soils
- Fix: Apply 2-4 tonnes/ha agricultural lime
- Crops that tolerate: cassava, yam, cowpea, sorghum, maize (with lime)

pH 5.5-6.0 (Slightly acidic):
- Acceptable for most crops with good fertilizer management
- Fix: Apply 1-2 tonnes/ha lime to raise to 6.0-6.5
- Most crops: groundnut, cassava, maize, soybean

pH 6.0-7.0 (Ideal):
- All nutrients available
- No lime needed
- All Nigerian crops grow well

pH 7.0-8.0 (Slightly alkaline):
- Iron and zinc can become deficient
- Common in Sokoto, Kebbi, Kano (arid zone soils)
- Fix: Apply sulfur at 200-500 kg/ha or use acidifying fertilizers (ammonium sulfate)
- Crops: Most cereals tolerate this. Vegetables need pH correction.

pH above 8.0 (Alkaline):
- Severe micronutrient deficiency
- Apply elemental sulfur, organic matter

How to test soil pH:
- State Ministry of Agriculture laboratory: N2,000-N5,000 per sample
- IITA soil testing service: Ibadan HQ
- Portable pH meter: N5,000-N20,000 for farm use
- Litmus paper strips: N500-N1,000 (rough guide only)
- Take samples from 10 spots in field, mix, test the mixture

SOIL NUTRIENT GUIDE

NITROGEN (N) - for leafy growth, dark green color
Deficiency signs: Yellow leaves starting from older leaves. Stunted growth. Pale green color.
Sources: Urea (46% N), CAN (26% N), NPK fertilizers, cow manure, green manure
Recommended dose: 60-150 kg N/ha depending on crop
Hausa name: Taki mai nitrogen

PHOSPHORUS (P) - for roots, flowers, and fruit
Deficiency signs: Purple or red coloring on leaves and stems. Delayed maturity. Poor root development.
Sources: Single Super Phosphate (18% P2O5), TSP (46% P2O5), NPK fertilizers, bone meal
Recommended dose: 40-80 kg P2O5/ha
Note: Phosphorus is often deficient in Nigerian soils especially red laterite soils.

POTASSIUM (K) - for strong stems, disease resistance, fruit quality
Deficiency signs: Brown scorched leaf edges, weak stems, poor fruit quality
Sources: Muriate of Potash (60% K2O), NPK fertilizers, wood ash
Recommended dose: 40-80 kg K2O/ha
Note: Very important for cassava, yam, potato, banana, plantain which are high K crops.

CALCIUM (Ca) - for cell walls, fruit quality
Deficiency signs: Blossom end rot in tomato and pepper, tip burn in lettuce, poor pod fill in groundnut
Sources: Agricultural lime (CaCO3), gypsum (CaSO4), dolomite
Recommended dose: Apply lime for pH correction - calcium provided automatically

MAGNESIUM (Mg) - for chlorophyll, photosynthesis
Deficiency signs: Yellowing between leaf veins (interveinal chlorosis) on older leaves
Sources: Dolomite lime, magnesium sulfate (Epsom salt), some NPK formulations
Recommended dose: 20-40 kg MgO/ha as foliar or soil application

ZINC (Zn) - for crop growth at all stages
Deficiency signs: Small leaves, shortened internodes, striped or mottled leaves, poor grain set
Sources: Zinc sulfate foliar spray (2g/L water), some micronutrient packages
Note: Very common in Nigerian soils especially those with pH above 7.0. Maize and rice very sensitive.

BORON (B) - for pollination and fruit set
Deficiency signs: Hollow stems, poor fruit set, distorted growth of growing points
Sources: Borax foliar spray (2g/L water)
Note: Important for tomato, pepper, groundnut, cassava flower/fruit set.

SOIL CONSERVATION AND EROSION CONTROL

Nigeria loses significant topsoil every year to erosion - especially in Anambra, Imo, Enugu (gully erosion), and Kogi, Kwara (sheet erosion). This is a serious threat to long-term farm productivity.

Erosion control methods:
1. Minimum tillage: Reduce frequency of ploughing. Use mulch.
2. Contour farming: Plant along contours not up and down slopes.
3. Grass strips: Plant vetiver grass strips every 20-30m across slope.
4. Cover cropping: Plant cowpea, mucuna, or other legumes as ground cover.
5. Agroforestry: Integrate trees (gliricidia, leucaena) in farm fields.
6. Check dams: Build small earth or stone dams in gullies.
7. Terracing: For steep slopes above 8 degrees.
8. Mulching: Maintain 5-10cm mulch cover at all times.

Soil organic matter improvement:
- Compost: Apply 5-10 tonnes/ha every season (most affordable)
- Manure: Cow dung 5-10 tonnes/ha, poultry manure 2-3 tonnes/ha (higher N)
- Green manure: Bury cowpea or soybean biomass before planting
- Crop residues: Incorporate maize stalks and other residues rather than burning
- Biochar: Apply charred organic matter at 2-5 tonnes/ha (long-lasting)

SOIL TESTING SERVICES IN NIGERIA

Federal level:
- IITA (International Institute of Tropical Agriculture), Ibadan - world-class soil lab
- NASC (National Agricultural Seeds Council) soil testing centres
- Federal University of Agriculture Abeokuta (FUNAAB) - Ogun
- Federal University of Agriculture Makurdi (FUAM) - Benue

State level:
- State Ministry of Agriculture - each state capital
- Agricultural Development Programme (ADP) offices - each LGA
- FADAMA offices - for fadama-specific soil testing

Private services:
- Soil Science Society of Nigeria - certified soil analysts
- Crop specific services: cocoa (CRIN Ibadan), cassava (IITA)
- Test cost: N2,000-N10,000 per sample (basic NPK + pH)
- Full micronutrient test: N15,000-N30,000 per sample

Soil test interpretation guide:
- Always test before applying lime or fertilizer
- Take samples at 0-20cm depth (topsoil) and 20-40cm (subsoil)
- Take minimum 10 cores per field and mix well
- Test every 3 years or after major yield decline
