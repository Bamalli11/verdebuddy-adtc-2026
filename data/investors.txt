NIGERIA AGRICULTURAL OVERVIEW FOR INVESTORS

Nigeria is Africa's largest economy and most populous nation with 220 million people. Agriculture contributes approximately 25% of GDP and employs over 70% of the rural workforce. Nigeria is a net food importer despite having 84 million hectares of arable land, of which only 40% is currently cultivated. This gap represents a massive investment opportunity.

Key investment drivers:
- Growing urban middle class driving demand for processed and premium foods
- Government's ARISE agenda prioritizing food security and agricultural export
- CBN Anchor Borrowers Programme providing credit to smallholder farmers
- AFCFTA (African Continental Free Trade Area) opening 1.3 billion consumer market
- Underutilized land and water resources across all geopolitical zones
- Strong diaspora remittance channel for agribusiness financing

Nigeria's top agricultural exports: sesame, cashew, cocoa, ginger, hibiscus (zobo), shea butter, rubber
Nigeria's top agricultural imports (opportunity gaps): wheat, rice, fish, dairy, tomato paste, sugar

TOP INVESTMENT OPPORTUNITIES BY SECTOR

RICE PRODUCTION AND MILLING
Investment size: N50 million - N5 billion
Current gap: Nigeria imports 2-3 million tonnes of rice annually worth over $1 billion
Government policy: 100% duty on rice imports (strong local market protection)
ROI timeline: 3-5 years for integrated farm + mill operation
Best locations: Kebbi (Argungu), Niger (Bida), Ebonyi, Benue, Taraba
Key players: Dangote Rice, Crown Flour Mills, Stallion Group
Entry strategy: Contract farming aggregation model or direct large-scale irrigation farming
Risks: Flooding, inconsistent power for milling, smuggling through borders
Estimated returns: 25-40% IRR for integrated operations
Land lease cost: N50,000 - N200,000 per hectare per year depending on state

TOMATO PROCESSING
Investment size: N200 million - N2 billion
Current gap: Nigeria imports N120 billion worth of tomato paste annually
Problem: 50% of fresh tomato harvest is lost post-harvest due to no processing
Opportunity: Build processing plants near Kano, Kaduna, Plateau production zones
ROI timeline: 4-6 years for processing plant
Key consideration: Cold chain logistics, generator backup for processing
Estimated returns: 30-45% IRR for well-managed processing plant
Land lease cost: Industrial land N500,000 - N2,000,000 per plot in processing zones

CASSAVA VALUE CHAIN
Investment size: N20 million - N500 million
Products: Garri, cassava flour, starch, ethanol, HQCF (High Quality Cassava Flour)
Current opportunity: Nigeria produces 60 million tonnes of cassava (world's largest) but exports little processed product
Government incentive: 10% cassava flour blending policy for wheat flour (mandatory)
Best entry: Garri processing cooperative + export to diaspora markets (UK, USA)
ROI timeline: 2-3 years for processing
Estimated returns: 20-35% ROI on processing operations

POULTRY (CHICKEN AND EGG)
Investment size: N5 million - N1 billion
Current gap: Nigeria produces 700,000 tonnes of poultry meat vs need of 1.5 million tonnes
Feed cost: Maize and soya are main costs - 70% of total cost
Best locations: Ogun, Kaduna, Plateau, Lagos hinterland (Ogun most popular)
ROI timeline: 12-18 months for broilers, 18-24 months for layers
Estimated returns: 25-35% ROI with good management
Key risks: Newcastle disease (vaccinate at 7, 21, 35 days), heat stress, feed price volatility
Land requirement: 1 hectare for 10,000 bird farm

FISH FARMING (CATFISH AND TILAPIA)
Investment size: N2 million - N500 million
Current gap: Nigeria imports $500 million of fish annually
Species: African catfish (Clarias) - most profitable. Tilapia - faster growing.
Production systems: Earthen ponds (cheapest), concrete tanks, recirculating aquaculture
Best locations: Delta, Rivers, Anambra, Cross River, Lagos, Ogun, Kogi
ROI timeline: 6-12 months per production cycle
Estimated returns: 30-50% ROI per cycle with good management
Feed: Major cost (60-70% of total). Local feed mills cheaper than imported.
Market: Fresh catfish commands N2,500-N4,500/kg at farm gate

GREENHOUSE VEGETABLE PRODUCTION
Investment size: N5 million - N200 million
Target crops: Tomato, pepper, cucumber, lettuce, herbs - premium and export quality
Markets: Hotels, supermarkets, restaurants in Lagos/Abuja, export to EU
Best locations: Ogun (proximity to Lagos), Kaduna, Plateau, FCT outskirts
ROI timeline: 2-3 years
Estimated returns: 40-60% ROI (high-value crops, premium pricing)
Key challenge: High setup cost, technical expertise for climate control
Land requirement: 1 hectare greenhouse serves N20-50 million market annually

EXPORT CROPS (HIGHEST PRIORITY FOR FOREIGN INVESTORS)
Sesame: Nigeria is world's 4th largest exporter. 100% export crop. Low input. Strong demand from Japan, China, Middle East.
Cashew: Strong EU and Asian demand for raw cashew nut. Kogi, Kwara, Oyo belt.
Ginger: Kaduna/Plateau/Nasarawa belt. Premium product for EU, Middle East markets.
Hibiscus (Zobo): Kano, Jigawa belt. EU food colorant demand growing.
Shea butter: North Nigeria. Cosmetics industry demand growing globally.
Cocoa: Ondo, Cross River. World 4th producer but falling yields - rehabilitation opportunity.

LAND ACQUISITION GUIDE FOR INVESTORS

Land tenure types in Nigeria:
1. Certificate of Occupancy (C of O) - strongest form, issued by state governor, renewable every 99 years
2. Right of Occupancy - issued by Local Government, less secure
3. Customary land - community owned, negotiate with community leaders and families
4. Federal land - available from Federal Ministry of Agriculture for large-scale farming

Land acquisition process:
Step 1: Identify land through Ministry of Agriculture or land agents
Step 2: Conduct due diligence - confirm no competing claims
Step 3: Pay land survey fees (N50,000 - N500,000 depending on size)
Step 4: Apply for C of O from State Ministry of Lands
Step 5: Pay ground rent (varies by state, typically N5,000 - N50,000/year)
Timeline: 6-18 months for full C of O
Cost: N100,000 - N2,000,000 for processing fees depending on state

Available farmland lease rates by zone:
North West (Kebbi, Sokoto, Kano): N30,000 - N80,000/ha/year (lowest)
North Central (Niger, Kwara, Nasarawa): N50,000 - N150,000/ha/year
North East (Gombe, Bauchi, Taraba): N30,000 - N100,000/ha/year
South West (Ogun, Oyo, Osun): N100,000 - N400,000/ha/year
South East (Enugu, Anambra): N150,000 - N500,000/ha/year
South South (Rivers, Delta): N200,000 - N800,000/ha/year (highest)

GOVERNMENT INCENTIVES AND PROGRAMMES

Federal Government incentives:
- Pioneer Status: 3-5 year tax holiday for agro-processing companies
- Agricultural Credit Guarantee Scheme (ACGS): CBN guarantees 75% of agricultural loans
- Anchor Borrowers Programme: Interest rate 9% per annum (below market rate)
- Agricultural Credit Support Scheme (ACSS): Up to N500 million at 6% for large farms
- Export Expansion Grant (EEG): Cash back on exported agricultural products
- NIRSAL (Nigeria Incentive-Based Risk Sharing for Agricultural Lending): De-risks agricultural finance

State government incentives:
- Most states offer free land to large-scale investors (minimum 500 hectares)
- Some states offer tax holidays on agricultural equipment and inputs
- States like Kebbi and Niger offer irrigation infrastructure at subsidized rates

Key regulatory bodies:
- NAFDAC: Food processing certification
- SON (Standards Organisation of Nigeria): Product quality standards
- NAQS (Nigerian Agricultural Quarantine Service): Export certification
- CAC (Corporate Affairs Commission): Business registration
- FIRS (Federal Inland Revenue Service): Tax compliance

Business registration timeline: 2-4 weeks via CAC online portal
Cost: N10,000 - N50,000 for company registration

INFRASTRUCTURE CONSIDERATIONS FOR INVESTORS

Power:
- National grid unreliable - budget for generator or solar backup
- Solar + battery systems now cost-competitive for medium farms (N5-20 million for 50kw system)
- Some states (Kebbi, Niger) have better grid access near irrigation schemes

Water:
- Rainfed agriculture: Follow rainfall maps (500-1500mm depending on zone)
- Irrigation: River Niger basin, Lake Chad basin, Hadejia-Nguru wetlands (North)
- Groundwater: Most available in middle belt and South. Borehole cost N1-3 million.
- Irrigation development: N500,000 - N2,000,000/hectare for drip system

Roads:
- North: Federal highways reasonable, rural roads poor
- South: Better road density but congestion near ports
- Rail: Abuja-Kaduna, Lagos-Ibadan, Lagos-Kano (new) - improving freight options

Ports:
- Lagos (Apapa, Tincan): Main export port. Congestion common.
- Onne (Rivers): Preferred for South-South agric exports
- Calabar: Good for Cross River/Enugu corridor
- Kano dry port: Links to North via rail

Cold chain:
- Major gap in Nigerian agric value chain
- Cold storage investment alongside fresh produce farm is high-ROI opportunity
- Government incentive available for cold chain infrastructure

RISK MANAGEMENT FOR AGRICULTURAL INVESTORS

Key risks and mitigation:
1. Weather/climate risk: Buy crop insurance (NAIC - Nigerian Agricultural Insurance Corporation). Cost: 5-8% of asset value annually.
2. Price volatility: Forward contracts with off-takers. AFEX warehouse receipt system.
3. Security: North East and North West have security concerns. Hire local security, work with community leaders. Middle Belt, South West generally safer for farming.
4. Currency risk: Export crops priced in USD provide natural hedge. Maintain USD account.
5. Regulatory risk: Work with NIPC (Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission) for investor protection.
6. Land disputes: Always get C of O. Engage community leaders before land acquisition.
7. Input supply chain: Build relationships with NOTORE, Olam, Dizengoff for fertilizer and seed supply.

Due diligence checklist for investors:
- Confirm land title (C of O or Right of Occupancy)
- Check water availability and source
- Verify road access to major markets
- Assess soil quality (take samples to Ministry of Agriculture lab)
- Survey local labor availability and cost
- Check proximity to power infrastructure
- Identify nearest market and off-taker
- Verify security situation with state government

MARKET ACCESS AND EXPORT INFORMATION

Major domestic markets:
- Dawanau (Kano): Largest grain market in West Africa
- Mile 12 (Lagos): Largest fresh produce market in Nigeria
- Makurdi (Benue): Yam and cassava hub
- Onitsha (Anambra): General commodity trade hub
- Maiduguri: North East hub

Export certification requirements:
- NAQS phytosanitary certificate (mandatory for all agricultural exports)
- NAFDAC certification for processed foods
- SON quality mark for packaged products
- GlobalGAP certification (required for EU vegetable exports - hire consultant)
- Organic certification: CERES, Control Union (premium price 20-40% above conventional)

Top export destinations and prices (2026):
- Sesame: China, Japan, India - $900-1,200/tonne FOB
- Cashew (RCN): Vietnam, India - $900-1,400/tonne FOB
- Ginger (dry): EU, USA, Middle East - $1,500-2,500/tonne FOB
- Hibiscus: EU (food colorant) - $2,000-3,500/tonne FOB
- Cocoa beans: EU, USA - $3,000-4,500/tonne FOB
- Shea butter: EU (cosmetics) - $1,200-2,000/tonne FOB
- Cashew kernels (processed): 3x price of raw cashew - major value addition opportunity

AGTECH AND DIGITAL AGRICULTURE INVESTMENT

Emerging opportunities in Nigerian agtech:
- Farm management software for smallholder farmers
- Digital commodity trading platforms
- Drone services for crop monitoring and spraying
- Precision agriculture and soil testing services
- Cold chain and logistics technology
- Agricultural insurance and index products
- Input finance and BNPL for farmers

Key agtech players in Nigeria:
- Releaf: Smallholder aggregation and processing
- Farmcrowdy: Crowdfunded farming
- ThriveAgric: Farmer financing and support
- Agrorite: Input supply marketplace
- AFEX: Commodity exchange and warehouse receipts

Investment entry options for foreign investors:
1. Direct farming: Full ownership, highest control, highest risk
2. Joint venture with Nigerian partner: Shared risk, local knowledge
3. Contract farming: Off-take agreements, lower capital, lower risk
4. Agribusiness equity: Invest in existing Nigerian agribusiness
5. Technology platform: Serve farmers digitally

Foreign ownership rules:
- 100% foreign ownership allowed in agriculture (no restriction)
- Must register with NIPC for investor protection
- Repatriation of profits allowed freely under NIPC Act
- No restriction on foreign currency accounts
